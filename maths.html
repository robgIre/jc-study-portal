6. Sets

Junior Cycle Maths — Strand: Statistics & Probability
A set is a collection of well-defined objects. You need to know set notation, Venn diagrams, and how to find unions, intersections and complements.

Venn Diagrams

Venn diagrams show sets as overlapping circles inside a rectangle (the universal set).

  • The overlapping part = intersection (A ∩ B)
  • The whole of both circles = union (A ∪ B)
  • The area outside a circle but inside the rectangle = complement (A')

The Key Formula (2 sets)

n(A ∪ B) = n(A) + n(B) − n(A ∩ B)
You subtract the intersection because it gets counted twice (once in A, once in B).

Worked Example

In a class of 30: 18 play football, 12 play hurling, 5 play both. How many play neither?

  • n(F ∪ H) = 18 + 12 − 5 = 25
  • Neither = 30 − 25 = 5 students

Set-Builder Notation (HL)

Instead of listing elements, describe the set using a rule:

  • {x : x > 3} means "the set of all x such that x is greater than 3"
  • {x : x ∈ ℕ, x < 8} = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7} (natural numbers less than 8)
  • The colon ":" means "such that"

3-Set Venn Diagrams (HL)

Work from the centre out: fill in the intersection of all three sets first, then the pairwise intersections, then the remaining parts of each circle, then outside all circles.

Worked Example

60 students were surveyed about sports. 25 play football (F), 20 play hurling (H), 22 play basketball (B). 8 play F and H, 6 play F and B, 5 play H and B, 3 play all three. How many play none?

  1. Centre (all three): 3
  2. F ∩ H only: 8 − 3 = 5
  3. F ∩ B only: 6 − 3 = 3
  4. H ∩ B only: 5 − 3 = 2
  5. F only: 25 − 5 − 3 − 3 = 14
  6. H only: 20 − 5 − 2 − 3 = 10
  7. B only: 22 − 3 − 2 − 3 = 14
  8. Total in at least one: 14 + 10 + 14 + 5 + 3 + 2 + 3 = 51
  9. None: 60 − 51 = 9

7. Patterns & Sequences

Junior Cycle Maths — Strand: Number
Finding rules in number and visual patterns, and writing formulas for the nth term.

Linear Sequences

A linear sequence increases (or decreases) by the same amount each time. This amount is called the common difference (d).

Example: 3, 7, 11, 15, 19, ...

  • Common difference: d = 4 (each term goes up by 4)
  • First term: a = 3

Finding the nth Term

nth term = dn + (a − d)
Where d = common difference, a = first term.
For the example above: T(n) = 4n + (3 − 4) = 4n − 1
Check: T(1) = 4(1) − 1 = 3 ✔   T(2) = 4(2) − 1 = 7 ✔

Steps to Find the nth Term

  1. Find the common difference (d) by subtracting consecutive terms.
  2. The coefficient of n is d.
  3. Subtract d from the first term to get the constant: a − d.
  4. Write the formula: T(n) = dn + (a − d).
  5. Always check by substituting n = 1 and n = 2.

Visual / Geometric Patterns

You'll be shown shapes that grow (e.g., matchstick patterns, dot patterns). The method is the same:

  1. Make a table: pattern number (n) vs. number of items.
  2. Find the first differences (how much each row goes up).
  3. If first differences are constant → it's linear. Use T(n) = dn + (a − d).
  4. If first differences aren't constant, find second differences (HL: quadratic).

Decreasing Sequences

Sequences can go down too. The method is exactly the same — the common difference will be negative.

Example: 20, 17, 14, 11, 8, ...

  • d = −3, a = 20
  • T(n) = −3n + (20 − (−3)) = −3n + 23
  • Check: T(1) = −3 + 23 = 20 ✔

Table of Values Method

The exam often gives you a table to complete. Fill in the table, then find the pattern:

n12345n
T(n)581114173n + 2
1st diff+3+3+3+3constant → linear

Quadratic Sequences (HL)

If the second differences are constant, the sequence is quadratic. The nth term has the form an² + bn + c. At JC HL you need to recognise these but the full formula isn't usually required — you'll extend the pattern instead.

8. Speed, Distance & Time

Junior Cycle Maths — Strand: Number
The relationship between speed, distance and time, unit conversions, and distance-time graphs.

The Triangle Formula

Distance = Speed × Time   |   Speed = Distance ÷ Time   |   Time = Distance ÷ Speed
Remember the DST triangle: D on top, S and T on the bottom. Cover the one you want — the other two show the formula.

Units

SpeedDistanceTime
km/hkmhours
m/smetresseconds

Unit Conversions

  • km/h → m/s: Divide by 3.6   (e.g., 72 km/h = 72 ÷ 3.6 = 20 m/s)
  • m/s → km/h: Multiply by 3.6   (e.g., 15 m/s = 15 × 3.6 = 54 km/h)
  • Time tip: 30 minutes = 0.5 hours, 45 minutes = 0.75 hours, 15 minutes = 0.25 hours

Average Speed

Average Speed = Total Distance ÷ Total Time
For multi-stage journeys, add up all distances and all times first, THEN divide.

Worked Example

A car drives 120 km in 2 hours, then 60 km in 1.5 hours. What's the average speed?

  • Total distance = 120 + 60 = 180 km
  • Total time = 2 + 1.5 = 3.5 hours
  • Average speed = 180 ÷ 3.5 = 51.4 km/h

Distance-Time Graphs

  • Flat line = stopped (no distance covered)
  • Steep line = fast speed
  • Gentle slope = slow speed
  • Line going down = returning / going back
  • Speed = gradient of the line (rise ÷ run)

9. Financial Maths

Junior Cycle Maths — Strand: Number
Percentages, profit and loss, VAT, interest, bills, currency, and best buys.

Percentages

  • Find % of an amount: Amount × (% ÷ 100). E.g., 15% of €240 = 240 × 0.15 = €36
  • Percentage increase: New = Original × (1 + rate). E.g., increase €80 by 20% = 80 × 1.20 = €96
  • Percentage decrease: New = Original × (1 − rate). E.g., decrease €80 by 20% = 80 × 0.80 = €64
  • Find the % change: (Change ÷ Original) × 100

Profit & Loss

  • Cost Price (CP) — what you pay for an item
  • Selling Price (SP) — what you sell it for
  • Profit = SP − CP (positive means profit, negative means loss)
  • % Profit = (Profit ÷ CP) × 100
  • % Loss = (Loss ÷ CP) × 100

Worked Example

A shop buys a phone for €200 and sells it for €260.

  • Profit = 260 − 200 = €60
  • % Profit = (60 ÷ 200) × 100 = 30%

VAT (Value Added Tax)

  • Adding VAT: Price × (1 + VAT rate). E.g., €100 + 23% VAT = 100 × 1.23 = €123
  • Finding price before VAT: Price incl. VAT ÷ (1 + VAT rate). E.g., €123 ÷ 1.23 = €100
  • Finding VAT amount: Price incl. VAT − Price excl. VAT

Simple Interest

I = P × R × T ÷ 100
I = Interest earned, P = Principal (starting amount), R = Rate (%), T = Time (years)

Worked Example

€2,000 invested for 3 years at 4% simple interest.

  • I = 2000 × 4 × 3 ÷ 100 = €240
  • Total after 3 years = 2000 + 240 = €2,240

Compound Interest (HL)

A = P(1 + r/100)n
A = Final amount, P = Principal, r = Rate (%), n = Number of years.
Interest is calculated on the NEW total each year (interest on interest).

Worked Example

€2,000 invested for 3 years at 4% compound interest.

  • Year 1: 2000 × 1.04 = €2,080
  • Year 2: 2080 × 1.04 = €2,163.20
  • Year 3: 2163.20 × 1.04 = €2,249.73
  • Or: A = 2000 × (1.04)³ = 2000 × 1.124864 = €2,249.73

Currency Conversion

  • To convert: Multiply by the exchange rate (check which direction!).
  • If €1 = $1.10, then €200 = 200 × 1.10 = $220
  • To go back: $220 ÷ 1.10 = €200

Depreciation (HL)

When something loses value over time (cars, computers). Works like compound interest but the value goes down.

A = P(1 − r/100)n
Same as compound interest but with a minus sign.

Worked Example

A car worth €20,000 depreciates by 15% per year. What is it worth after 3 years?

  • A = 20000 × (1 − 0.15)³ = 20000 × (0.85)³ = 20000 × 0.614125 = €12,282.50

Successive Percentage Changes

When a price goes up by one percentage and then down by another (or vice versa), you cannot just subtract the percentages.

Example: A €200 item increases by 10% then decreases by 10%.

  • After increase: 200 × 1.10 = €220
  • After decrease: 220 × 0.90 = €198 (NOT €200!)

The result is not the original price because the decrease is applied to the new, higher amount.

Reading Bills

Exam questions may show an electricity, phone, or gas bill. Key things to look for:

  • Standing charge — a fixed daily/monthly charge regardless of usage
  • Unit rate — the price per unit of electricity/gas used (e.g., cent per kWh)
  • Total before VAT = standing charge + (units used × unit rate)
  • Total with VAT = total × (1 + VAT rate)
  • Read the bill carefully — some have day rates and night rates (different prices)

Worked Example

Standing charge: €10/month. Rate: 18c per kWh. Usage: 350 kWh. VAT: 13.5%.

  • Cost = €10 + (350 × €0.18) = €10 + €63 = €73
  • With VAT: €73 × 1.135 = €82.86

Best Buys (Unit Pricing)

To compare value, calculate the price per unit (per gram, per litre, per item) for each option. The lowest unit price is the best buy.

Example

500g of pasta for €1.20 vs. 750g for €1.65

  • 500g: €1.20 ÷ 500 = €0.0024/g
  • 750g: €1.65 ÷ 750 = €0.0022/g ← better value

10. Area & Volume

Junior Cycle Maths — Strand: Geometry & Trigonometry
Formulas for 2D area and 3D volume. Learn the formulas on the next page — they come up every year.

ParallelogramA = bhbase × perpendicular height (NOT the slant side)

  • Circumference (perimeter of circle) = 2πr = πd
  • Diameter = 2 × radius
  • Use π = 3.14159... or the π button on your calculator

Volume Formulas (3D Shapes)

ShapeFormulaNotes
CuboidV = l × w × hlength × width × height

CylinderV = πr²hArea of circle base × height

Surface Area

  • Cuboid: SA = 2(lw + lh + wh) — six rectangles, three pairs
  • Cylinder: SA = 2πrh + 2πr² — curved surface + two circular ends
  • Sphere (HL): SA = 4πr²
  • Tip: Draw the net (unfolded shape) to see all the faces clearly.

Perimeter

Perimeter = the total distance around the outside of a shape. Add up all the sides.

  • Rectangle: P = 2(l + w)
  • Square: P = 4s
  • Circle (circumference): C = 2πr = πd
  • Semicircle: C = πr + 2r (half the circumference + the diameter)
  • Compound shapes: Add the outer edges only — don't include internal boundaries.

Answers "In Terms of π"

When the exam says "give your answer in terms of π", do NOT multiply by 3.14. Leave π as a symbol in your answer.

Example: Area of a circle with radius 5 cm

  • A = πr² = π(5)² = 25π cm² ✔
  • NOT 78.54 cm² ✘

This gives an exact answer rather than a rounded decimal.

Compound Shapes

For irregular shapes, split them into simpler shapes (rectangles, triangles, semicircles), calculate each area separately, then add (or subtract) them.

Cone & Pyramid volume tip: Both are ⅓ of their "parent" shape. Cone = ⅓ cylinder. Pyramid = ⅓ prism. Just remember the ⅓.

11. Algebra

Junior Cycle Maths — Strand: Algebra & Functions
Using letters to represent numbers. Simplify, expand, factorise, and solve equations.

Key Rules

  • Like terms: 3x + 5x = 8x  (same letter → add the numbers)
  • Unlike terms: 3x + 2y — can't be simplified (different letters)
  • Multiplying: 2x × 3x = 6x²  (multiply numbers, add powers)
  • Dividing: 6x² ÷ 2x = 3x  (divide numbers, subtract powers)

Expanding Brackets

Single bracket:

Multiply each term inside by the term outside.

  • 3(2x + 5) = 6x + 15
  • −2(x − 4) = −2x + 8  (watch the signs!)

Double brackets (HL):

Use FOIL: First, Outside, Inside, Last.

  • (x + 3)(x + 5) = x² + 5x + 3x + 15 = x² + 8x + 15

Factorising

Common factor (OL & HL):

Find what's common to all terms and take it out.

  • 6x + 9 = 3(2x + 3)
  • 4x² + 8x = 4x(x + 2)

Quadratic factorising (HL):

x² + bx + c → find two numbers that multiply to c and add to b.

  • x² + 7x + 12 = (x + 3)(x + 4)  (3 × 4 = 12, 3 + 4 = 7)

Difference of two squares (HL):

a² − b² = (a + b)(a − b)
E.g., x² − 25 = (x + 5)(x − 5)

Substitution

Replace letters with numbers and calculate the result. Follow BIMDAS (Brackets, Indices, Multiply, Divide, Add, Subtract).

Example: If a = 3 and b = −2, find the value of 2a² − 3b

  • = 2(3)² − 3(−2)
  • = 2(9) − (−6)
  • = 18 + 6 = 24

Watch out: (−2)² = 4 (positive!), but −2² = −4. Brackets matter!

Forming Equations from Word Problems

Read the problem, assign a letter (usually x) to the unknown, write the equation, then solve.

Example: "I think of a number, double it, and add 5. The answer is 19. What's the number?"

  1. Let the number = x
  2. Equation: 2x + 5 = 19
  3. 2x = 14
  4. x = 7

Example: "A rectangle's length is 3 more than its width. The perimeter is 26 cm. Find the dimensions."

  1. Let width = x, so length = x + 3
  2. Perimeter: 2(x + x + 3) = 26
  3. 2(2x + 3) = 26 → 4x + 6 = 26 → 4x = 20 → x = 5
  4. Width = 5 cm, Length = 8 cm

Solving Linear Equations

Goal: get the letter on one side, numbers on the other.

Example: 3x + 7 = 22

  1. 3x = 22 − 7 = 15
  2. x = 15 ÷ 3 = 5

Example with brackets: 2(x + 3) = 14

  1. 2x + 6 = 14  (expand)
  2. 2x = 8
  3. x = 4

Example with x on both sides: 5x − 3 = 2x + 9

  1. 5x − 2x = 9 + 3
  2. 3x = 12
  3. x = 4

Simultaneous Equations (HL)

Two equations, two unknowns. Find x and y that satisfy both.

Elimination method:

Make the coefficients of one variable the same, then add/subtract.

Example: 2x + y = 7  &  x − y = 2

  1. Add the equations: 3x = 9, so x = 3
  2. Sub x = 3 into first equation: 2(3) + y = 7, so y = 1
  3. Answer: x = 3, y = 1

Inequalities

Solved exactly like equations, but the answer is a range of values, not a single number.

SymbolMeaning
<Less than
>Greater than
≤Less than or equal to
≥Greater than or equal to

Example: Solve 3x − 2 > 10

  1. 3x > 12
  2. x > 4

On a number line: open circle at 4 (not included), arrow pointing right.

If it was x ≥ 4: filled circle at 4 (included), arrow pointing right.

Key rule: If you multiply or divide both sides by a negative number, you must flip the inequality sign.
E.g., −2x > 6 → x < −3 (sign flips!)

Changing the Subject of a Formula (HL)

Rearrange a formula to isolate a different variable. Use the same rules as solving equations.

Example: Make r the subject of A = πr²

  1. r² = A ÷ π
  2. r = √(A/π)

Example: Make t the subject of v = u + at

  1. v − u = at
  2. t = (v − u) ÷ a

12. Constructions

Junior Cycle Maths — Strand: Geometry & Trigonometry
These must be done with a compass and straight edge ONLY (no protractor). You must show all construction arcs. Practice these until they're automatic.

Required Constructions

#ConstructionKey Steps
1Bisect a line segmentOpen compass more than half the line. Arc from each endpoint. Join the two crossing points. This also gives you a perpendicular bisector.
2Bisect an angleArc from the vertex to cut both arms. Arcs from those two points to cross each other. Join vertex to crossing point.
3Perpendicular from a point to a lineArc from the point to cut the line in two places. Bisect the segment between those two points.
4Perpendicular at a point on a lineMark equal distances each side of the point on the line. Bisect that segment.
5Angle of 60°Draw a ray. Open compass to any length. Arc from the endpoint. Without changing compass, arc from where first arc hits the ray. Join to get 60°.
6Angle of 30°Construct 60°, then bisect it.
7Angle of 90°Construct 60°, then construct another 60° from the first, and bisect the second 60°. Or use perpendicular bisector.
8Copy a given angleArc from vertex of original angle. Same compass width, arc from new vertex. Measure the chord of the original arc. Transfer that chord to the new arc. Join.
9Divide a line into equal partsDraw a ray at an angle. Mark equal steps with compass. Join last mark to end of original line. Draw parallels through each mark.
10Triangle (SSS, SAS, ASA)SSS: draw base, arc from each end with the other two sides. SAS: draw base, construct angle, mark length. ASA: draw base, construct both angles.
11Circumscribed circle (circumcircle)Bisect any two sides of the triangle. Where the bisectors meet = circumcentre. Open compass from centre to any vertex → draw circle.
12Inscribed circle (incircle)Bisect any two angles of the triangle. Where the bisectors meet = incentre. Perpendicular from centre to any side = radius → draw circle.
Exam Tips for Constructions
  • Always show your arcs — no arcs = no marks.
  • Use a sharp pencil and keep your compass tight.
  • Don't rub out construction lines — they're part of the answer.
  • Circumcircle: bisect SIDES (perpendicular bisectors meet at circumcentre).
  • Incircle: bisect ANGLES (angle bisectors meet at incentre).
  • Remember: circumcircle goes OUTSIDE (through vertices), incircle goes INSIDE (touches sides).
Circum vs In: Circumference goes around the outside → Circumcircle passes through the vertices (outside). Inside → Incircle sits inside touching the sides.

Maths Exam-Style Questions

SETS
In a group of 40 students, 22 study French, 18 study German, and 8 study both. How many study neither? Draw a Venn diagram.
Method:
  • n(F ∪ G) = 22 + 18 − 8 = 32
  • Neither = 40 − 32 = 8
  • Venn: F only = 14, G only = 10, both = 8, outside = 8
PATTERNS
A pattern produces these values: 7, 12, 17, 22, 27... (a) Find the common difference. (b) Write the nth term formula. (c) Find T(20).
Method:
  • (a) d = 5
  • (b) T(n) = 5n + (7−5) = 5n + 2
  • (c) T(20) = 5(20) + 2 = 102
SPEED, DISTANCE, TIME
A cyclist travels 30 km at 20 km/h, then 45 km at 30 km/h. Find (a) the total time, (b) the average speed for the whole journey.
Method:
  • (a) Time 1 = 30÷20 = 1.5 hrs. Time 2 = 45÷30 = 1.5 hrs. Total = 3 hrs.
  • (b) Avg speed = 75÷3 = 25 km/h
FINANCIAL MATHS
€5,000 is invested at 3.5% compound interest for 4 years. Find the final amount and the total interest earned.
Method:
  • A = 5000 × (1.035)&sup4; = 5000 × 1.14752 = €5,737.62
  • Interest = 5737.62 − 5000 = €737.62
AREA & VOLUME
A cylindrical tank has radius 3m and height 5m. Find (a) the volume and (b) the total surface area. Give answers in terms of π.
Method:
  • (a) V = π(3)²(5) = 45π m³
  • (b) SA = 2π(3)(5) + 2π(3)² = 30π + 18π = 48π m²
ALGEBRA
Solve the simultaneous equations: 3x + 2y = 16 and x − y = 2.
Method:
  • From equation 2: x = y + 2
  • Sub into equation 1: 3(y+2) + 2y = 16 → 3y + 6 + 2y = 16 → 5y = 10 → y = 2
  • x = 2 + 2 = 4. Answer: x = 4, y = 2.
CONSTRUCTIONS
Construct a triangle with sides 8cm, 6cm, and 5cm. Then construct the circumscribed circle of the triangle.
Steps:
  • Draw base 8cm. Arc of 6cm from one end, arc of 5cm from other end. Join.
  • Perpendicular bisector of any two sides. Where they meet = circumcentre.
  • Compass from circumcentre to any vertex → draw circle.
