# Science of Learning — Research Summary for JC Study Portal

## 32 papers reviewed across 5 areas. Key findings below.

---

## THE BIG FIVE FINDINGS (ranked by evidence strength)

### 1. Practice Testing (d = 0.50–0.72) — THE most powerful learning tool
- Roediger & Karpicke (2006): Students who tested themselves remembered 56% after a week vs 42% for re-readers
- Rowland (2014) meta-analysis of 159 studies: d = 0.50 overall, d = 0.56 with feedback
- Adesope et al (2017) meta-analysis of 272 effect sizes: g = 0.60 overall, g = 0.72 with feedback
- Butler (2010): Testing produced better TRANSFER to new questions, not just recall of tested facts
- Karpicke & Blunt (2011): Retrieval practice outperformed concept mapping — 67% vs 45% recall

### 2. Spaced Repetition (d = 0.42–1.05) — Spacing beats cramming every time
- Ebbinghaus (1885): 50% forgotten in 1 hour, 70% in 24 hours, 90% in a week without review
- Cepeda et al (2006) meta-analysis of 254 effect sizes: d = 0.42–1.05 depending on conditions
- Optimal gap: 10-20% of the retention interval (test in 30 days → study every 3-6 days)
- Kornell (2009): Spaced flashcard study = 46% vs 36% for cramming
- Rawson & Dunlosky (2011): 3 correct retrievals is optimal before moving on, then space it

### 3. Immediate Specific Feedback (d = 0.73) — One of the most powerful influences
- Hattie (2009): d = 0.73 across 800+ meta-analyses, 80 million students
- Not just "wrong" but "wrong — here's why, and here's the correct approach"
- Rowland (2014): Testing WITH feedback d = 0.56, WITHOUT feedback d = 0.33

### 4. Interleaving (d = 0.42–0.83) — Mix topics, don't block them
- Rohrer, Dedrick & Stershic (2015): 7th graders scored 72% interleaved vs 38% blocked (d = 0.83)
- Rohrer & Taylor (2007): Shuffled maths problems = 43% vs 20% for blocked
- Works because it forces students to discriminate between problem types

### 5. Worked Examples (d = 0.57–1.0) — Show the process, not just the answer
- Sweller & Cooper (1985): Students learned faster from worked examples than problem-solving (5x faster)
- Strongest for novice learners; diminishes with expertise (Kalyuga et al, 2003)
- Chi et al (1989): Self-explanation during worked examples adds d = 0.55

---

## SECONDARY FINDINGS

### Dual Coding — Words + Pictures together
- Paivio (1986): Recall ~2x better when both verbal and visual channels activated
- Mayer (2009): Spatial contiguity effect d = 1.10 (keep text near its diagram)
- Applies to ALL students, not just "visual learners"

### Generation Effect — Making answers, not reading them
- Slamecka & Graf (1978): Self-generated items remembered better, d = 0.40
- Free recall > MCQ > re-reading for learning (but MCQ still works well)

### Metacognition — Students are poor judges of what they know
- Dunlosky & Rawson (2012): Students predicted 70% retention but achieved 40%
- Low performers are the most overconfident (Kruger & Dunning, 1999)
- Delayed judgments of learning are accurate (r = 0.90) vs immediate (r = 0.10)
- Antidote: frequent low-stakes testing gives accurate self-knowledge

### Exam Anxiety — Expressive writing reduces it
- Ramirez & Beilock (2011): Writing about worries for 10 min before exam improved scores by ~0.5 grade points
- Strongest for highly anxious students

### Cognitive Load — Don't overload working memory
- Sweller (1988): Working memory holds ~4-7 items. Reduce extraneous load.
- Present ONE concept at a time. Progressive disclosure.
- Mayer's segmenting principle (d = 0.79): Learner-paced segments beat continuous

## WHAT DOESN'T WORK

| Strategy | Evidence | Why Students Use It |
|----------|----------|-------------------|
| Re-reading | d ≈ 0.05–0.15 (Callender & McDaniel, 2009) | Feels fluent, creates illusion of learning |
| Highlighting | d ≈ 0.0 (Dunlosky 2013) | Feels productive, actually passive |
| Learning styles matching | d ≈ 0.0 (Pashler et al, 2008) | Widely taught myth |
| Cramming | Works short-term, fails long-term | Feels efficient under time pressure |

### The Performance-Learning Paradox (Soderstrom & Bjork, 2015)
Strategies that feel EASY during study produce the WORST long-term learning.
Strategies that feel HARD during study produce the BEST long-term learning.
Students will prefer features that feel easy. The portal must actively guide them toward difficulty.

## DESIGN PRINCIPLES (derived from research)

### Tier 1 — Must Have
1. Practice testing / quizzes as PRIMARY activity
2. Immediate specific feedback on every answer
3. Spaced repetition for flashcards (Leitner system, graduate after 3 correct)
4. Worked examples with step-by-step explanations

### Tier 2 — High Value
5. Interleaved practice ("Surprise Me" mixing topics)
6. Dual coding (text + diagrams together)
7. Self-explanation prompts ("Why does this work?")
8. Study planner with spacing guidance
9. Weak topic tracking (based on quiz performance, not self-assessment)

### Tier 3 — Good to Have
10. Free recall (type answers) where feasible
11. Timed practice (exam simulation)
12. Concrete real-world examples
13. Confidence calibration

### Do NOT Build
- Learning style modes
- Highlighting features
- "Read again" as suggested action
- Passive browsing without retrieval
