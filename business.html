1. Household Finance

How households manage money — income, expenditure, and budgets. Budget questions appear almost every year.

Types of Income

TypeMeaningExamples
Regular incomeMoney received on a predictable, recurring basisWages, salary, Child Benefit, pension, rental income
Irregular incomeMoney that doesn't arrive on a set scheduleBonus, overtime, inheritance, lottery win, birthday money, commission
Wages vs Salary Wages = paid per hour/day/week, amount can vary. Salary = fixed annual amount, paid monthly. A factory worker earns wages; a teacher earns a salary.

Types of Expenditure

TypeMeaningExamples
Fixed expenditureSame amount every month, must be paidMortgage/rent, insurance, loan repayment, broadband
Discretionary (irregular) expenditureCan be changed or cut — spending by choiceHolidays, entertainment, eating out, new clothes, hobbies
Irregular expenditureUnexpected or one-off costsCar repairs, medical bills, broken appliance, vet bills

Household Budget

A budget is a financial plan that lists expected income and expenditure over a period (usually a month).

Key Budget Formulas

Net Cash = Total Income − Total Expenditure
Closing Cash = Opening Cash + Net Cash
A positive net cash means surplus. A negative net cash means deficit.

Why Prepare a Budget?

  • Helps plan spending and avoid debt
  • Shows if income covers all expenses
  • Identifies areas where spending can be cut
  • Helps save for future goals

2. Taxation

How the government raises money through taxes and what it spends it on.

Direct Tax vs Indirect Tax

Direct TaxIndirect Tax
Paid directly to the government by the personAdded to the price of goods/services — paid by the consumer but collected by the seller
Based on income or wealthBased on spending
Income Tax (PAYE), USC, PRSI, Motor Tax, LPTVAT, Excise Duty, Customs Duty, Carbon Tax, Sugar Tax

Key Tax Terms

  • PAYE (Pay As You Earn) — income tax deducted from your pay by your employer
  • Tax credits — amounts that reduce the total tax you owe
  • USC (Universal Social Charge) — a percentage tax on gross income
  • PRSI (Pay Related Social Insurance) — funds social welfare benefits
  • VAT (Value Added Tax) — added to the price of most goods and services (standard rate 23%)
  • Excise duty — extra tax on alcohol, tobacco, and fuel
  • LPT (Local Property Tax) — annual tax based on the value of your home

How PAYE is Calculated

Step 1: Calculate gross tax (income × tax rate)
Step 2: Subtract tax credits = Tax payable
Step 3: Also deduct USC and PRSI for total deductions
Net pay = Gross pay − Total deductions

Worked Example

Sarah earns €35,000 per year. Tax rate is 20%. Her annual tax credits are €3,550. What is her annual income tax?

  • Gross tax: €35,000 × 20% = €7,000
  • Tax payable: €7,000 − €3,550 = €3,450
  • Monthly tax: €3,450 ÷ 12 = €287.50

Government Income Sources

  • Income tax (PAYE, USC, PRSI) — the biggest source
  • VAT on goods and services
  • Excise duties on alcohol, tobacco, fuel
  • Corporation tax on company profits
  • EU grants and funding
  • Fees and fines
  • Sale of state assets

Government Spending

  • Health (HSE, hospitals)
  • Education (schools, universities)
  • Social welfare (jobseeker's, pensions, Child Benefit)
  • Housing and infrastructure
  • Defence
  • Servicing the national debt

3. Bookkeeping & Accounts

How businesses record transactions. The Income Statement and Analysed Cash Book come up regularly.

The Income Statement (Trading, Profit & Loss Account)

Shows whether a business made a profit or loss over a period.

Cost of Sales = Opening Stock + Purchases − Closing Stock
Gross Profit = Sales − Cost of Sales
Net Profit = Gross Profit − Expenses

Worked Income Statement

Income Statement for year ending 31 Dec 2025
Sales€120,000
Less Cost of Sales:
  Opening Stock€8,000
  + Purchases€65,000
  − Closing Stock(€10,000)
Cost of Sales€63,000
Gross Profit€57,000
Less Expenses:
  Rent€12,000
  Wages€18,000
  Insurance€3,000
  Light & heat€2,500
  Advertising€1,500
Total Expenses€37,000
Net Profit€20,000

Analysed Cash Book

Records all money coming in (debit/left side) and going out (credit/right side), with columns analysed by type.

  • Debit side (left) = receipts (money IN): sales, lodgements, refunds received
  • Credit side (right) = payments (money OUT): purchases, expenses, wages
  • Each entry shows date, details, total amount and is analysed into columns

VAT in the Cash Book

To find VAT on an item (at 23%): Price incl. VAT ÷ 1.23 = Price excl. VAT. Then VAT = Total − Excl. VAT.
Example: Item costs €123 incl. VAT. Excl. VAT = €123 ÷ 1.23 = €100. VAT = €23.

Ledger Accounts

Individual accounts for each type of income or expense. Entries are posted from the cash book to the relevant ledger.

  • Each ledger has a debit (left) and credit (right) side
  • The ledger is the opposite of the cash book: a debit entry in the cash book is posted to the credit side of the relevant ledger, and vice versa

Trial Balance

A list of all ledger balances. The total of debit balances must equal the total of credit balances.

Total Debits = Total Credits
If they don't balance, check for: figures entered on wrong side, arithmetic errors, missing entries, figures transposed.
Cost of Sales memory aid: Opening stock + Purchases − Closing stock = "OPC" — think "Open, Purchase, Close."

5. Employment

Employee and employer rights and responsibilities, pay structures, and the difference between gross and net pay.

Employee Rights

  • Right to a written contract of employment
  • Right to fair pay (at least the minimum wage)
  • Right to safe working conditions
  • Right to join a trade union
  • Right to no discrimination
  • Right to holidays and breaks
  • Right to a payslip

Employee Responsibilities

  • Do an honest day's work
  • Be punctual and reliable
  • Follow health and safety rules
  • Show loyalty to the employer
  • Honour the terms of their contract
  • Treat colleagues and customers with respect

Employer Rights & Responsibilities

Employer RightsEmployer Responsibilities
Expect honest work from employeesPay fair wages on time
Set reasonable rules and proceduresProvide a safe workplace
Dismiss employees for serious misconductGive a written contract
Expect loyalty and confidentialityNot discriminate in hiring or promotions
Provide training
Deduct and pay PAYE, PRSI, USC to Revenue

Types of Pay

Monetary Rewards

  • Wages — paid per hour/day/week
  • Salary — fixed annual amount paid monthly
  • Overtime — extra pay for hours beyond normal (time-and-a-half or double time)
  • Bonus — one-off payment for good performance
  • Commission — percentage of sales made

Non-Monetary Rewards (Benefits in Kind)

  • Company car, health insurance, gym membership, pension contributions, staff discounts, flexible working

Gross Pay vs Net Pay

Gross Pay = Total pay before any deductions
Net Pay = Gross Pay − Deductions (PAYE + USC + PRSI + pension)
Net pay is your "take-home pay".

Worked Example: Gross Pay Calculation

Mark works 40 hours at €14/hour. He does 6 hours overtime at time-and-a-half and 4 hours at double time. He also earns a €100 bonus.

Basic pay: 40 × €14€560.00
Overtime (time-and-a-half): 6 × €21€126.00
Overtime (double time): 4 × €28€112.00
Bonus€100.00
Gross Pay€898.00
Overtime rates: Time-and-a-half = normal rate × 1.5. Double time = normal rate × 2.

6. Insurance

Why we insure, types of insurance, and the principles behind it.

Why We Need Insurance

Insurance protects against financial loss from unexpected events. You pay a small regular amount (premium) to avoid a potentially huge cost.

Key Insurance Terms

TermMeaning
PremiumThe amount you pay for insurance cover
PolicyThe written contract between you and the insurance company
ClaimA request for payment after a loss
CoverWhat the policy protects you against
ExcessThe first part of a claim you must pay yourself
No claims bonusA discount for not making claims
LoadingAn extra charge added to your premium (e.g., for young drivers)

Types of Insurance

TypeWhat it covers
Motor insuranceDamage to your car, third-party damage, theft, personal injury
House insuranceBuildings and/or contents against fire, flood, theft, storm
Health insuranceMedical costs — hospital stays, consultations, treatments
Life insurancePays out a lump sum to your family if you die
Travel insuranceLost luggage, flight cancellations, medical emergencies abroad
Public liabilityCovers a business if a customer is injured on their premises
Employer's liabilityCovers a business if an employee is injured at work

Principles of Insurance

  • Indemnity — the insurance company puts you back in the same financial position as before the loss. You can't profit from insurance.
  • Utmost good faith — both sides must be completely honest.
  • Insurable interest — you can only insure something you would suffer a financial loss from.

Unlimited liability (sole traders, partnerships): Owner's personal assets can be taken to pay debts.

9. Consumer Rights

What protects you when you buy goods or services, and what to do when things go wrong.

Consumer Rights (under the Consumer Rights Act 2022)

When you buy goods, they must be:

  • Of satisfactory quality
  • Fit for purpose
  • As described
  • Match the sample

If goods are faulty, you are entitled to:

  • Repair, Replacement, or Refund

When You Are NOT Entitled to a Refund

  • You simply changed your mind
  • You were told about the fault before buying
  • You caused the damage yourself
  • You bought the item too long ago

Consumer Responsibilities

  • Keep your receipt, shop around, read labels, don't impulse buy, know your rights, report faults promptly, check your change

Online Shopping Rights

  • 14-day cooling-off period for online purchases
  • Seller must display name, address, contact details
  • Total price including delivery must be shown before you pay

Small Claims Court

Handles claims up to €2,000. Costs €25. No solicitor needed. Can be done online.

CCPC — Competition and Consumer Protection Commission

The government body that protects consumers. Investigates complaints, provides information, takes legal action, promotes competition.

10. International Trade

Why countries trade, visible and invisible trade, and the role of MNCs and the EU.

Why Countries Trade

  • No country can produce everything it needs
  • Some countries produce certain goods more cheaply
  • More choice for consumers
  • Creates jobs in exporting industries
  • Improves relations between countries

Visible vs Invisible Trade

Visible TradeInvisible Trade
What?Physical goods you can see and touchServices — can't see or touch
ImportsCars, oil, clothing bought from abroadIrish tourists spending money abroad
ExportsBeef, dairy, pharmaceuticals sold abroadSoftware services, tourism income
Balance of Trade = Visible Exports − Visible Imports
Exports > imports = trade surplus. Imports > exports = trade deficit.

Multinational Corporations (MNCs)

Why MNCs come to Ireland:

  • Low corporation tax (12.5%)
  • EU single market access
  • English-speaking, well-educated workforce
  • Government grants from IDA Ireland
  • Good infrastructure, stable politics

Why MNCs might leave:

  • Cheaper labour elsewhere, tax rate increases, grants ended, company restructuring

Benefits of EU Membership for Ireland

  • Free trade, EU funding, free movement, consumer protection, the Euro, access to 450+ million people

11. Economics

Supply and demand, inflation, interest rates, and the economic cycle.

Supply and Demand

Demand = quantity consumers are willing to buy at a given price.
Supply = quantity producers are willing to sell at a given price.

Law of Demand

As price goes up, demand goes down (and vice versa).

Law of Supply

As price goes up, supply goes up (and vice versa).

Factors Affecting Demand

  • Price, consumer income, price of substitutes, fashion/trends, advertising, population

Factors Affecting Supply

  • Cost of production, technology, government regulations, weather, number of suppliers

Inflation

Inflation is a general rise in the price of goods and services over time.

Causes of Inflation

  • Demand-pull — too much demand for too few goods
  • Cost-push — higher production costs passed on as higher prices

Effects of Inflation

  • Savings lose value, workers demand higher wages, fixed-income earners struggle, exports become expensive

Interest Rates

If interest rates GO UPIf interest rates GO DOWN
Saving more attractiveSaving less attractive
Borrowing more expensiveBorrowing cheaper
People spend lessPeople spend more

Scarcity & Opportunity Cost

  • Scarcity — resources are limited but wants are unlimited.
  • Opportunity cost — the next best alternative you give up when making a choice.

The Economic Cycle

  1. Boom — growth, low unemployment, high spending, rising prices
  2. Recession — slowing, businesses closing, unemployment rising
  3. Depression — severe and prolonged recession
  4. Recovery — economy begins to grow again

Simple Interest

Interest = Principal × Rate × Time ÷ 100

13. Sustainability & Ethics

How businesses and consumers can act responsibly towards the environment and society.

Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)

CSR means a business goes beyond just making profit and considers its impact on employees, the community, and the environment.

Examples of CSR:

Environmental Costs of Business

Sustainable Practices

For Consumers:
