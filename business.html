1. Household Finance

Junior Cycle Business Studies
How households manage money — income, expenditure, and budgets. Budget questions appear almost every year.

Types of Income

TypeMeaningExamples
Regular incomeMoney received on a predictable, recurring basisWages, salary, Child Benefit, pension, rental income
Irregular incomeMoney that doesn't arrive on a set scheduleBonus, overtime, inheritance, lottery win, birthday money, commission
Wages vs Salary Wages = paid per hour/day/week, amount can vary. Salary = fixed annual amount, paid monthly. A factory worker earns wages; a teacher earns a salary.

Types of Expenditure

TypeMeaningExamples
Fixed expenditureSame amount every month, must be paidMortgage/rent, insurance, loan repayment, broadband
Discretionary (irregular) expenditureCan be changed or cut — spending by choiceHolidays, entertainment, eating out, new clothes, hobbies
Irregular expenditureUnexpected or one-off costsCar repairs, medical bills, broken appliance, vet bills

Household Budget

A budget is a financial plan that lists expected income and expenditure over a period (usually a month).

Key Budget Formulas

Net Cash = Total Income − Total Expenditure
Closing Cash = Opening Cash + Net Cash
A positive net cash means the household has money left over (surplus). A negative net cash means overspending (deficit).

Why Prepare a Budget?

  • Helps plan spending and avoid debt
  • Shows if income covers all expenses
  • Identifies areas where spending can be cut
  • Helps save for future goals

2. Taxation

Junior Cycle Business Studies
How the government raises money through taxes and what it spends it on.

Direct Tax vs Indirect Tax

Direct TaxIndirect Tax
Paid directly to the government by the personAdded to the price of goods/services — paid by the consumer but collected by the seller
Based on income or wealthBased on spending
Income Tax (PAYE), USC, PRSI, Motor Tax, LPT (Local Property Tax)VAT, Excise Duty, Customs Duty, Carbon Tax, Sugar Tax

Key Tax Terms

  • PAYE (Pay As You Earn) — income tax deducted from your pay by your employer before you receive it
  • Tax credits — amounts that reduce the total tax you owe (e.g., Single Person's Tax Credit, Employee Tax Credit)
  • USC (Universal Social Charge) — a percentage tax on gross income
  • PRSI (Pay Related Social Insurance) — funds social welfare benefits (jobseeker's, pension, maternity)
  • VAT (Value Added Tax) — added to the price of most goods and services (standard rate 23%)
  • Excise duty — extra tax on alcohol, tobacco, and fuel
  • LPT (Local Property Tax) — annual tax based on the value of your home

How PAYE is Calculated

Step 1: Calculate gross tax (income × tax rate)
Step 2: Subtract tax credits = Tax payable
Step 3: Also deduct USC and PRSI for total deductions
Net pay = Gross pay − Total deductions

Worked Example

Sarah earns €35,000 per year. Tax rate is 20%. Her annual tax credits are €3,550. What is her annual income tax?

  • Gross tax: €35,000 × 20% = €7,000
  • Tax payable: €7,000 − €3,550 = €3,450
  • Monthly tax: €3,450 ÷ 12 = €287.50

Government Income Sources

  • Income tax (PAYE, USC, PRSI) — the biggest source
  • VAT on goods and services
  • Excise duties on alcohol, tobacco, fuel
  • Corporation tax on company profits
  • EU grants and funding
  • Fees and fines (e.g., court fines, licence fees)
  • Sale of state assets (e.g., shares in state companies)

Government Spending

  • Health (HSE, hospitals)
  • Education (schools, universities)
  • Social welfare (jobseeker's, pensions, Child Benefit)
  • Housing and infrastructure (roads, public transport)
  • Defence (army, navy)
  • Servicing the national debt (interest on borrowings)

3. Bookkeeping & Accounts

Junior Cycle Business Studies
How businesses record transactions. The Income Statement and Analysed Cash Book come up regularly.

The Income Statement (Trading, Profit & Loss Account)

Shows whether a business made a profit or loss over a period (usually one year).

Cost of Sales = Opening Stock + Purchases − Closing Stock
Gross Profit = Sales − Cost of Sales
Net Profit = Gross Profit − Expenses

Analysed Cash Book

Records all money coming in (debit/left side) and going out (credit/right side), with columns analysed by type.

  • Debit side (left) = receipts (money IN): sales, lodgements, refunds received
  • Credit side (right) = payments (money OUT): purchases, expenses, wages
  • Each entry shows date, details, total amount and is analysed into columns (e.g., Purchases, VAT, Wages, Expenses)

VAT in the Cash Book

To find VAT on an item (at 23%): Price incl. VAT ÷ 1.23 = Price excl. VAT. Then VAT = Total − Excl. VAT.
Example: Item costs €123 incl. VAT. Excl. VAT = €123 ÷ 1.23 = €100. VAT = €23.

Ledger Accounts

Individual accounts for each type of income or expense (e.g., Sales Ledger, Purchases Ledger, Wages Account). Entries are posted from the cash book to the relevant ledger.

  • Each ledger has a debit (left) and credit (right) side
  • The ledger is the opposite of the cash book: a debit entry in the cash book is posted to the credit side of the relevant ledger, and vice versa

Trial Balance

A list of all ledger balances. The total of debit balances must equal the total of credit balances. If they don't match, there's an error somewhere.

Total Debits = Total Credits
If they don't balance, check for: figures entered on wrong side, arithmetic errors, missing entries, figures transposed (e.g., €540 written as €450).
Cost of Sales memory aid: Opening stock + Purchases − Closing stock = "OPC" — think "Open, Purchase, Close."

5. Employment

Junior Cycle Business Studies
Employee and employer rights and responsibilities, pay structures, and the difference between gross and net pay.

Employee Rights

  • Right to a written contract of employment
  • Right to fair pay (at least the minimum wage — €12.70/hour in 2024)
  • Right to safe working conditions (Health & Safety)
  • Right to join a trade union
  • Right to no discrimination (based on gender, age, race, religion, disability, etc.)
  • Right to holidays and breaks
  • Right to a payslip

Employee Responsibilities

  • Do an honest day's work
  • Be punctual and reliable
  • Follow health and safety rules
  • Show loyalty to the employer
  • Honour the terms of their contract
  • Treat colleagues and customers with respect

Employer Rights & Responsibilities

Employer RightsEmployer Responsibilities
Expect honest work from employeesPay fair wages on time
Set reasonable rules and proceduresProvide a safe workplace
Dismiss employees for serious misconductGive a written contract
Expect loyalty and confidentialityNot discriminate in hiring or promotions
Provide training
Deduct and pay PAYE, PRSI, USC to Revenue

Types of Pay

Monetary Rewards

  • Wages — paid per hour/day/week (can include overtime)
  • Salary — fixed annual amount paid monthly
  • Overtime — extra pay for hours worked beyond normal (often time-and-a-half or double time)
  • Bonus — one-off payment for good performance
  • Commission — percentage of sales made (common in sales jobs)

Non-Monetary Rewards (Benefits in Kind)

  • Company car
  • Health insurance
  • Gym membership
  • Pension contributions
  • Staff discounts
  • Flexible working hours / work from home

Gross Pay vs Net Pay

Gross Pay = Total pay before any deductions (basic pay + overtime + bonus + commission)
Net Pay = Gross Pay − Deductions (PAYE + USC + PRSI + pension)
Net pay is your "take-home pay" — what actually goes into your bank account.

Worked Example: Gross Pay Calculation

Mark works 40 hours at €14/hour. He does 6 hours overtime at time-and-a-half and 4 hours at double time. He also earns a €100 bonus this week.

Basic pay: 40 × €14€560.00
Overtime (time-and-a-half): 6 × (€14 × 1.5) = 6 × €21€126.00
Overtime (double time): 4 × (€14 × 2) = 4 × €28€112.00
Bonus€100.00
Gross Pay€898.00
Overtime rates: Time-and-a-half = normal rate × 1.5. Double time = normal rate × 2. Triple time = normal rate × 3.

6. Insurance

Junior Cycle Business Studies
Why we insure, types of insurance, and the principles behind it.

Why We Need Insurance

Insurance protects against financial loss from unexpected events. You pay a small regular amount (premium) to avoid a potentially huge cost if something goes wrong.

Key Insurance Terms

TermMeaning
PremiumThe amount you pay (monthly/annually) for insurance cover
PolicyThe written contract between you and the insurance company
ClaimA request to the insurance company for payment after a loss
CoverWhat the policy protects you against
ExcessThe first part of a claim you must pay yourself (e.g., first €250)
No claims bonusA discount on your premium for not making claims
LoadingAn extra charge added to your premium (e.g., for young drivers)

Types of Insurance

TypeWhat it covers
Motor insuranceDamage to your car, third-party damage, theft, personal injury
House insuranceBuildings (structure) and/or contents (belongings) against fire, flood, theft, storm
Health insuranceMedical costs — hospital stays, consultations, treatments
Life insurancePays out a lump sum to your family if you die
Travel insuranceLost luggage, flight cancellations, medical emergencies abroad
Public liabilityCovers a business if a customer is injured on their premises
Employer's liabilityCovers a business if an employee is injured at work

Principles of Insurance

  • Indemnity — the insurance company will put you back in the same financial position you were in before the loss. You can't profit from insurance.
  • Utmost good faith — both sides must be completely honest. If you lie on your application (e.g., hide a medical condition), the policy can be cancelled.
  • Insurable interest — you can only insure something you would suffer a financial loss from. You can insure your own car but not your neighbour's car.

Exam Skill: Identifying Insurance Types from Scenarios

Typical exam question: "Name the type of insurance needed in each case."
A family going on holiday to Spain = Travel insurance
A shop worker slips on a wet floor = Employer's liability
A tree falls on your roof = House (buildings) insurance
A driver crashes into another car = Motor insurance

Limited vs Unlimited Liability

Unlimited liability (sole traders, partnerships): If the business owes money, the owner's personal assets (house, car, savings) can be taken to pay debts.
Limited liability (Ltd, PLC): Shareholders can only lose the amount they invested. Personal assets are protected.

The Business Plan

A document prepared before starting a business. It helps the entrepreneur think through the idea and is needed to get a bank loan or grant.

What's in a business plan:

  • Business idea / description of product or service
  • Target market and market research findings
  • Marketing plan (how you will attract customers)
  • Financial plan — start-up costs, projected income, cash flow forecasts
  • Location and premises
  • Staffing requirements
  • Sources of finance (own savings, bank loan, grants)

Impact of Business on a Community

  • Jobs — reduces local unemployment
  • Multiplier effect — workers spend wages locally, boosting other businesses
  • Sponsorship — local teams and events
  • Services — provides goods/services people need
  • Taxes — contributes to government revenue
  • Potential negatives — traffic, noise, pollution, competition for local shops

  • Digital: Social media (TikTok, Instagram), websites, Google ads, email marketing, influencer marketing
  • Other: Sponsorship of events/teams, product placement in films/TV

Sales Promotion Methods

  • Competitions and prize draws
  • Loyalty cards (e.g., Dunnes stores card)
  • Discounts and sales (e.g., Black Friday)
  • Free samples or trial periods
  • BOGOF (Buy One Get One Free)

  • Gift with purchase

Product Life Cycle

Every product goes through stages:

  1. Introduction — new product launched, sales low, high promotion costs
  2. Growth — sales increasing rapidly, profits begin
  3. Maturity — sales peak and level off, competition strong
  4. Decline — sales fall, product may be withdrawn or updated

Extension strategies (to delay decline): new packaging, target new market, add features, reduce price, new advertising campaign.

Branding & Packaging

  • A brand is the name, logo, or design that identifies a product (e.g., Nike swoosh, Apple logo)
  • Good branding creates customer loyalty and allows higher prices
  • Packaging protects the product, provides information (ingredients, instructions), and attracts customers
The 4 Ps: Product, Price, Place, Promotion. All four must work together — a great product at a wrong price in the wrong place with no promotion will fail.

9. Consumer Rights

Junior Cycle Business Studies
What protects you when you buy goods or services, and what to do when things go wrong.

Consumer Rights (under the Consumer Rights Act 2022)

When you buy goods, they must be:

  • Of satisfactory quality — work properly, no defects
  • Fit for purpose — do what they're supposed to do
  • As described — match the description on the label or advertisement
  • Match the sample — same as any sample shown to you

If goods are faulty, you are entitled to:

  • Repair of the product
  • Replacement with an identical product
  • Refund (full or partial)

When You Are NOT Entitled to a Refund

  • You simply changed your mind (shops may offer exchange as goodwill but don't have to)
  • You were told about the fault before buying (e.g., shop sold it at a reduced price because of a scratch)
  • You caused the damage yourself through misuse
  • You bought the item too long ago

Consumer Responsibilities

  • Always keep your receipt (proof of purchase)
  • Shop around — compare prices before buying
  • Read labels and contracts carefully
  • Don't impulse buy — think before spending
  • Know your rights — you can't exercise rights you don't know about
  • Report faulty goods promptly to the seller
  • Check your change and receipts

Online Shopping Rights

  • You have a 14-day cooling-off period for online purchases — you can return for any reason within 14 days of receiving the item
  • The seller must clearly display their name, address, and contact details
  • The total price including delivery charges must be shown before you pay
  • Same consumer rights apply as in-store (goods must be as described, fit for purpose, satisfactory quality)

Small Claims Court

If a shop refuses to give a refund/replacement for a genuinely faulty item, you can take a case to the Small Claims Court.

  • Handles claims up to €2,000
  • Costs €25 to lodge a claim
  • No solicitor needed — designed to be simple
  • Can be done online at courts.ie

CCPC — Competition and Consumer Protection Commission

The government body that protects consumers. It:

  • Investigates complaints
  • Provides information on consumer rights
  • Takes legal action against businesses that break consumer law
  • Promotes competition in the market

10. International Trade

Junior Cycle Business Studies
Why countries trade, visible and invisible trade, and the role of MNCs and the EU.

Why Countries Trade

  • No country can produce everything it needs
  • Some countries can produce certain goods more cheaply or efficiently
  • Trade gives consumers more choice
  • Creates jobs in exporting industries
  • Improves relations between countries

Visible vs Invisible Trade

Visible TradeInvisible Trade
What?Physical goods you can see and touchServices — you can't see or touch them
ImportsCars, oil, clothing, electronics bought from abroadIrish tourists spending money abroad, foreign consultants hired
ExportsBeef, dairy, pharmaceuticals, medical devices sold abroadSoftware services, tourism income from foreign visitors, financial services
Balance of Trade = Visible Exports − Visible Imports
If exports > imports = trade surplus (good).
If imports > exports = trade deficit (bad).

Multinational Corporations (MNCs)

Large companies that operate in more than one country (e.g., Apple, Google, Pfizer, Intel).

Why MNCs come to Ireland:

  • Low corporation tax (12.5% — one of the lowest in Europe)
  • Access to the EU single market (500+ million customers)
  • English-speaking, well-educated workforce
  • Government grants from IDA Ireland
  • Good infrastructure and technology
  • Stable political environment

Why MNCs might leave:

  • Found cheaper labour/costs elsewhere
  • Tax rates increased
  • Government grants ended
  • Brexit or other trade disruptions
  • Company restructuring globally

Benefits of EU Membership for Ireland

  • Free trade — no tariffs or customs between EU countries
  • EU funding — grants for infrastructure, farming (CAP), research
  • Free movement — Irish people can live and work in any EU country
  • Consumer protection laws across the EU
  • The Euro — single currency makes trade easier, no exchange rate costs
  • Access to a market of 450+ million people

11. Economics

Junior Cycle Business Studies
Supply and demand, inflation, interest rates, and the economic cycle.

Supply and Demand

Demand = the quantity of a product consumers are willing to buy at a given price.
Supply = the quantity of a product producers are willing to sell at a given price.

Law of Demand

As price goes up, demand goes down (and vice versa). People buy less when things are expensive.

Law of Supply

As price goes up, supply goes up (and vice versa). Producers want to sell more when they can charge higher prices.

Factors Affecting Demand

  • Price of the good
  • Consumer income (more income = more spending)
  • Price of substitutes (if Coke goes up, people buy Pepsi)
  • Fashion and trends
  • Advertising
  • Population changes

Factors Affecting Supply

  • Cost of production (raw materials, wages)
  • Technology improvements (makes production cheaper)
  • Government regulations/taxes
  • Weather (for agriculture)
  • Number of suppliers in the market

Inflation

Inflation is a general rise in the price of goods and services over time. It means your money buys less than before.

Causes of Inflation

  • Demand-pull — too much demand for too few goods pushes prices up
  • Cost-push — higher production costs (wages, raw materials, energy) are passed on as higher prices

Effects of Inflation

  • Savings lose value (your money buys less)
  • Workers demand higher wages
  • Fixed-income earners (pensioners) struggle
  • Irish goods become more expensive abroad (hurts exports)
  • Borrowers benefit (they repay loans in money that's worth less)

Interest Rates

Interest rate = the cost of borrowing money, or the reward for saving it.

If interest rates GO UPIf interest rates GO DOWN
Saving becomes more attractive (higher returns)Saving becomes less attractive
Borrowing becomes more expensiveBorrowing becomes cheaper
People spend less → demand fallsPeople spend more → demand rises
Mortgage repayments go upMortgage repayments go down

Scarcity & Opportunity Cost

  • Scarcity — resources are limited but wants are unlimited. We can't have everything.
  • Opportunity cost — the next best alternative you give up when making a choice. If you spend €50 on a game, you can't also spend that €50 on clothes — the clothes are the opportunity cost.

The Economic Cycle

  1. Boom — economy growing fast, low unemployment, high spending, rising prices
  2. Recession — economy slowing, businesses closing, unemployment rising, spending falls
  3. Depression — severe and prolonged recession
  4. Recovery — economy begins to grow again, jobs created, confidence returns

  • Retirement
  • Avoid getting into debt

13. Sustainability & Ethics

Junior Cycle Business Studies
How businesses and consumers can act responsibly towards the environment and society.

Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)

CSR means a business goes beyond just making profit and considers its impact on employees, the community, and the environment.

Examples of CSR:

  • Reducing carbon emissions and waste
  • Using recyclable or biodegradable packaging
  • Paying fair wages and treating workers well
  • Supporting local charities and community projects
  • Sourcing materials ethically (e.g., Fairtrade)
  • Offering flexible working arrangements

Environmental Costs of Business

  • Pollution — air, water, and noise pollution from factories and transport
  • Waste — packaging, electronic waste, food waste
  • Resource depletion — using up raw materials (forests, minerals, fossil fuels)
  • Carbon footprint — the total greenhouse gases produced by a business

Sustainable Practices

For Businesses:

  • Switch to renewable energy (solar, wind)
  • Reduce, reuse, recycle in operations
  • Use sustainable raw materials
  • Minimise packaging
  • Encourage remote working (less commuting)
  • Set carbon reduction targets

For Consumers:

  • Buy local and seasonal products
  • Use the deposit return scheme for cans and bottles
  • Bring reusable bags, bottles, cups
  • Choose Fairtrade and ethically sourced products
  • Reduce food waste
  • Buy second-hand where possible

Key Sustainability Terms

TermMeaning
FairtradeEnsures farmers/producers in developing countries get a fair price for their goods
Ethical consumerismBuying products based on their social/environmental impact, not just price
Carbon footprintTotal amount of greenhouse gases produced directly or indirectly by a person/business
Deposit Return SchemePay a small deposit on cans/bottles, get it back when you return them for recycling
Circular economyProducts are designed to be reused, repaired, or recycled rather than thrown away
GreenwashingWhen a company pretends to be environmentally friendly without actually making real changes

Benefits of Recycling

  • Reduces waste going to landfill
  • Conserves natural resources
  • Uses less energy than making products from scratch
  • Reduces pollution and greenhouse gas emissions
  • Creates jobs in recycling industries

Gross Profit = Sales − Cost of Sales. It's profit before expenses are deducted.
Documents
What is a credit note used for?
Sent by the seller to reduce the amount owed, usually because goods were returned or damaged.
Documents
What is the difference between trade discount and cash discount?
Trade discount = given to business buyers who buy in bulk (deducted before VAT). Cash discount = given for paying early.
Employment
What is the difference between gross pay and net pay?
Gross = total pay before deductions. Net = take-home pay after PAYE, USC, PRSI, and pension are deducted.
Employment
What is time-and-a-half?
Overtime rate = normal hourly rate × 1.5. E.g., if normal rate is €14, time-and-a-half = €21.
Insurance
What does "indemnity" mean in insurance?
The insurance company puts you back in the same financial position as before the loss. You can't profit from a claim.
Insurance
What is "utmost good faith"?
Both the insurer and the insured must be completely honest. Lying on your application can void the policy.
Enterprise
What is limited liability?
Shareholders can only lose the money they invested. Their personal assets (house, car) are protected if the business fails.
Enterprise
Name the four factors of production.
Land (natural resources), Labour (workers), Capital (money/equipment), Enterprise (the entrepreneur).
Marketing
What are the 4 Ps of Marketing?
Product, Price, Place, Promotion. All four must work together for marketing to succeed.
Marketing
Name the four stages of the product life cycle.
Introduction, Growth, Maturity, Decline. Extension strategies can delay decline.
Consumer
When are you NOT entitled to a refund?
If you changed your mind, were told about the fault before buying, or caused the damage yourself.
Trade
What is a visible export?
A physical good sold to another country (e.g., Irish beef sold to France). "Visible" because you can see and touch it.
Economics
What is opportunity cost?
The next best alternative you give up when you make a choice. E.g., if you buy a game instead of clothes, the clothes are the opportunity cost.
Economics
Name two causes of inflation.
Demand-pull (too much demand, not enough supply) and cost-push (higher production costs passed on as higher prices).
Financial Institutions
Difference between a current account and a savings account?
Current = everyday spending, debit card, little interest. Savings = putting money aside, earns interest, limited access.
Sustainability
What is greenwashing?
When a company pretends to be environmentally friendly without actually making real changes. Misleading marketing about environmental practices.
Sustainability
What is CSR?
Corporate Social Responsibility — a business goes beyond profit and considers its impact on employees, community, and environment.
Trade
Why do MNCs come to Ireland?
Low corporation tax (12.5%), EU single market access, English-speaking educated workforce, IDA grants, good infrastructure.

Exam-Style Questions with Worked Answers

HOUSEHOLD FINANCE — BUDGET
The Evans family has the following monthly figures. Complete their budget: Mother's salary €2,600, Father's wages €1,900, Child Benefit €280. Expenditure: Mortgage €1,200, Groceries €700, Car insurance €180, Electricity €150, Entertainment €250, Clothing €100, Broadband €60. Opening cash: €350. Find the net cash and closing cash.
Solution:
  • Total Income = 2,600 + 1,900 + 280 = €4,780
  • Total Expenditure = 1,200 + 700 + 180 + 150 + 250 + 100 + 60 = €2,640
  • Net Cash = 4,780 − 2,640 = €2,140 (surplus)
  • Closing Cash = 350 + 2,140 = €2,490
BOOKKEEPING — INCOME STATEMENT
Prepare an Income Statement from the following: Sales €95,000. Opening Stock €6,000. Purchases €48,000. Closing Stock €7,000. Expenses: Wages €15,000, Rent €8,000, Insurance €2,000, Advertising €1,200. Find Cost of Sales, Gross Profit, and Net Profit.
Solution:
  • Cost of Sales = 6,000 + 48,000 − 7,000 = €47,000
  • Gross Profit = 95,000 − 47,000 = €48,000
  • Total Expenses = 15,000 + 8,000 + 2,000 + 1,200 = €26,200
  • Net Profit = 48,000 − 26,200 = €21,800
EMPLOYMENT — GROSS PAY
Lisa works 38 hours at €15/hour. She does 5 hours overtime at time-and-a-half and 3 hours at double time. She also earns commission of 2% on €4,000 of sales. Calculate her gross pay.
Solution:
  • Basic pay: 38 × €15 = €570
  • Time-and-a-half: 5 × (€15 × 1.5) = 5 × €22.50 = €112.50
  • Double time: 3 × (€15 × 2) = 3 × €30 = €90
  • Commission: 2% of €4,000 = €80
  • Gross Pay = 570 + 112.50 + 90 + 80 = €852.50
BUSINESS DOCUMENTS — INVOICE
Murphy's Supplies sends an invoice for: 30 desks at €80 each and 60 chairs at €35 each. Trade discount 15%. VAT 23%. Calculate the total amount due.
Solution:
  • Desks: 30 × €80 = €2,400
  • Chairs: 60 × €35 = €2,100
  • Subtotal = €4,500
  • Trade discount: 15% of €4,500 = €675
  • Net total = €4,500 − €675 = €3,825
  • VAT: 23% of €3,825 = €879.75
  • Total due = €3,825 + €879.75 = €4,704.75
TAXATION
(a) Explain the difference between direct and indirect tax. Give two examples of each. (b) Tom earns €30,000. Tax rate: 20%. Annual tax credits: €3,300. Calculate his annual income tax and his monthly take-home pay (ignore USC/PRSI for this question).
Solution:
  • (a) Direct tax: paid directly to the government based on income (e.g., PAYE, PRSI). Indirect tax: added to prices, paid when you buy goods (e.g., VAT, excise duty).
  • (b) Gross tax: €30,000 × 20% = €6,000
  • Tax payable: €6,000 − €3,300 = €2,700 per year
  • Monthly tax: €2,700 ÷ 12 = €225
  • Monthly gross: €30,000 ÷ 12 = €2,500
  • Monthly take-home: €2,500 − €225 = €2,275
MARKETING
A new café is opening in your town. Using the marketing mix (4 Ps), outline a marketing strategy for the first month.
Sample answer:
  • Product: High-quality coffee, fresh pastries, sandwiches. Offer a unique selling point (e.g., locally roasted coffee, student meal deal).
  • Price: Competitive with nearby cafés. Introductory offer — 20% off in the first week. Loyalty card — every 10th coffee free.
  • Place: Located on a busy street near schools/offices. Available on Deliveroo/Just Eat for takeaway orders.
  • Promotion: Instagram and TikTok page. Flyers distributed locally. Grand opening event with free samples. Local newspaper ad.
INTERNATIONAL TRADE
(a) Explain the difference between visible and invisible imports and exports. Give one example of each. (b) Ireland exported €85 billion of goods and imported €62 billion of goods last year. Calculate the Balance of Trade and state whether this is a surplus or deficit.
Solution:
  • (a) Visible = physical goods (exports: Irish beef sold abroad; imports: oil bought from Saudi Arabia). Invisible = services (exports: Irish software services sold abroad; imports: Irish tourists spending money in Spain).
  • (b) Balance of Trade = €85bn − €62bn = €23 billion surplus.
  • It is a surplus because exports are greater than imports.
CONSUMER RIGHTS
Mary bought a pair of shoes online. They arrived and the sole came off after two days. (a) What are Mary's rights? (b) What should she do? (c) If she bought the shoes in a shop and changed her mind the next day (nothing wrong with them), is she entitled to a refund?
Solution:
  • (a) The shoes are not of satisfactory quality and not fit for purpose. Mary is entitled to a repair, replacement, or refund under the Consumer Rights Act.
  • (b) Contact the seller (not the manufacturer), explain the problem, provide proof of purchase (email receipt/order confirmation), request a remedy.
  • (c) No. If there is nothing wrong with the goods and she simply changed her mind, the shop does not have to give a refund. Some shops may offer an exchange or credit note as goodwill.
