1. The Reformation & Martin Luther

1517 – 1563
The Reformation was a religious revolution that split Western Christianity into Catholic and Protestant. It began with Martin Luther's challenge to the Catholic Church and transformed Europe forever.

Background: Problems in the Catholic Church

By the early 1500s, many people were unhappy with the Catholic Church. Key abuses included:

  • Simony — Buying and selling Church positions (bishoprics, etc.)
  • Absenteeism — Clergy not living in the area they were supposed to serve
  • Pluralism — Holding multiple Church positions at the same time
  • Nepotism — Giving Church jobs to family members
  • Sale of Indulgences — Paying money to the Church to reduce time in Purgatory. Johann Tetzel was a famous seller: "As soon as the coin in the coffer rings, the soul from Purgatory springs."

Martin Luther (1483–1546)

  • Born in Saxony, Germany. Became an Augustinian monk and theology professor at the University of Wittenberg.
  • Was outraged by Tetzel selling indulgences near Wittenberg.
  • On 31 October 1517, he nailed his Ninety-Five Theses (arguments against indulgences) to the door of the Castle Church in Wittenberg.
  • Thanks to the printing press (invented by Gutenberg, c.1440), his ideas spread rapidly across Europe.

Luther's Key Beliefs

  • Salvation by faith alone — Good works alone cannot save you. Only faith in God can.
  • The Bible is the sole authority — Not the Pope. Luther translated the Bible into German so ordinary people could read it.
  • Priesthood of all believers — All Christians are equal; you don't need a priest as a go-between with God.
  • Only two sacraments (Baptism and Eucharist), not seven.
  • Clergy should be allowed to marry.

What Happened to Luther?

  • 1520: Pope Leo X issued a Papal Bull (official letter) threatening to excommunicate Luther. Luther burned it publicly.
  • 1521: Luther was excommunicated. He was summoned to the Diet of Worms (an assembly, not food!) by Emperor Charles V. Luther refused to take back his ideas: "Here I stand. I can do no other."
  • He was declared an outlaw but was protected by Frederick the Wise of Saxony and hidden in Wartburg Castle, where he translated the New Testament into German.

The Spread of Protestantism

  • Lutheranism spread through Germany and Scandinavia.
  • John Calvin founded Calvinism in Geneva, Switzerland. He believed in predestination (God has already decided who is saved).
  • Henry VIII broke from Rome in 1534 (not for religious reasons but because the Pope wouldn't annul his marriage to Catherine of Aragon). He became head of the Church of England (Anglican Church).
  • John Knox brought Calvinism to Scotland (Presbyterianism).

The Counter-Reformation (Catholic Response)

The Catholic Church launched its own reform movement:

  • Council of Trent (1545–1563) — A series of meetings that reformed abuses (banned simony, indulgence selling), reaffirmed Catholic doctrine (seven sacraments, Pope's authority, good works AND faith needed).
  • The Jesuits — Founded by Ignatius of Loyola in 1534. A religious order focused on education and missionary work. They helped win back lands for Catholicism.
  • The Inquisition — A court set up to find and punish heresy (beliefs against Church teaching).
  • Index of Forbidden Books — A list of books Catholics were banned from reading.
Exam Tip: "Account" Questions If asked to give an "account of the Reformation," structure your answer: (1) Problems in the Church, (2) Luther's actions, (3) His beliefs, (4) How it spread, (5) Counter-Reformation. Hit all five and you're sorted.

2. The Plantations in Ireland

1556 – 1652
Plantations involved confiscating Irish land and giving it to loyal English and Scottish settlers (planters). The aim was to control Ireland and spread Protestantism. The Ulster Plantation (1609) was the most significant.

Why Did the English Plant Ireland?

  • Political control — Ireland was seen as a backdoor for England's enemies (Spain, France) to attack.
  • Religious control — Ireland remained Catholic after the Reformation. England wanted to spread Protestantism.
  • Economic gain — Irish land and resources were valuable.
  • Punish rebellion — After Irish rebellions, land was confiscated as punishment.

The Main Plantations

1. Laois-Offaly Plantation (1556) — Mary I

  • First official plantation. Counties renamed King's County (Offaly) and Queen's County (Laois).
  • The O'Moore and O'Connor clans were removed from their lands.
  • Result: Failed. Not enough planters came. The Irish kept attacking settlers.

2. Munster Plantation (1586) — Elizabeth I

  • After the Desmond Rebellions in Munster, 600,000 acres were confiscated.
  • Undertakers were granted large estates and had to bring in English tenants, build defences and ban Irish tenants.
  • Famous planter: Sir Walter Raleigh (also brought potatoes and tobacco to Ireland).
  • Result: Partially failed. Not enough English settlers. Many undertakers employed Irish workers (cheaper). Destroyed in the Nine Years' War (1594–1603).

3. Ulster Plantation (1609) — James I (MOST IMPORTANT)

  • After the Flight of the Earls (1607) — Hugh O'Neill and Rory O'Donnell fled to Europe — their lands in six Ulster counties were confiscated.
  • Counties: Donegal, Derry, Tyrone, Armagh, Cavan, Fermanagh.
Three Types of Grantees:
  • Undertakers — English/Scottish Protestants. Got the best land (up to 2,000 acres). Had to bring in Protestant settlers and build a castle or bawn (fortified enclosure). Could NOT have Irish tenants.
  • Servitors — Former English soldiers/officials. Got medium-quality land. COULD have some Irish tenants.
  • Native Irish — Got the worst, least productive land. Had to pay higher rent and follow English law and customs.

Success of the Ulster Plantation:

  • More successful than earlier plantations — large numbers of Scottish Presbyterians settled in Ulster.
  • Derry was given to London guilds and renamed Londonderry. They built walls around the city (still standing today).
  • New towns were built with market squares, churches, and schools.
  • However, many undertakers still secretly hired Irish workers (cheaper labour).
  • Irish were pushed onto poor land — created deep resentment that led to the 1641 Rebellion.

4. Cromwellian Plantation (1652)

  • After Oliver Cromwell's brutal conquest of Ireland (1649–1652), massive land confiscation.
  • The Act of Settlement (1652): Catholic landowners were sent "To Hell or to Connacht" — forced west of the Shannon.
  • Land was given to Cromwell's soldiers and financial backers (Adventurers).
  • By 1660, Catholic ownership of land fell from 60% to about 8–9%.

Consequences of the Plantations

  • Land ownership: Catholics lost almost all their land.
  • Religious divide: Protestant settlers vs. Catholic Irish — a divide that still affects Northern Ireland.
  • Cultural change: English language, law, and customs replaced Irish traditions in planted areas.
  • Resentment: Irish bitterness led to rebellions in 1641, 1798, and beyond.
  • New towns: Planned towns with market squares, churches, and streets built by planters.
Remember the 4 Plantations in order: Laois-Offaly, Munster, Ulster, Cromwellian — "Lazy Monks Usually Complain"

3. The American Revolution

1765 – 1783
The thirteen American colonies fought for and won their independence from Britain. The revolution was driven by unfair taxation and a desire for self-government. Its ideas of liberty and equality inspired revolutions in France and Ireland.

Background: The Thirteen Colonies

  • By the 1760s, Britain controlled 13 colonies along the east coast of North America.
  • After the Seven Years' War (1756–1763) against France, Britain had huge debts and decided to tax the American colonists to pay for it.
  • The colonists had no representatives in the British Parliament — they had no say in these taxes.

Causes of the Revolution

  • "No taxation without representation" — The colonists' rallying cry. They argued it was unjust to be taxed by a parliament they had no voice in.
  • Stamp Act (1765) — Tax on all printed documents (newspapers, legal papers, playing cards). Colonists were furious. Repealed in 1766.
  • Townshend Acts (1767) — Taxes on glass, paint, paper, and tea. Colonists boycotted British goods.
  • Boston Massacre (1770) — British soldiers fired on a crowd of protesters in Boston, killing 5 people. Used as propaganda against the British.
  • Tea Act (1773) & Boston Tea Party — Britain gave the East India Company a monopoly on tea sales. In protest, colonists dressed as Native Americans boarded ships and dumped 342 chests of tea into Boston Harbour.
  • Intolerable Acts (1774) — Britain's punishment: closed Boston port, banned town meetings, forced colonists to house British soldiers. This united the colonies against Britain.

Key Events of the War

April 1775 — Battles of Lexington and Concord: first shots of the war ("the shot heard round the world"). The American War of Independence begins.
July 1776 — Declaration of Independence signed (mainly written by Thomas Jefferson). Declared "all men are created equal" with rights to "life, liberty and the pursuit of happiness."
1777–78 — Terrible winter at Valley Forge (Pennsylvania). Washington's army suffered but survived, emerging stronger.
1778 — France entered the war on the American side (revenge against Britain). Provided troops, ships, and money.
October 1781 — Battle of Yorktown: the last major battle. British General Cornwallis surrendered to Washington. The war was effectively over.
1783 — Treaty of Paris: Britain officially recognised American independence.

Results & Importance

  • The United States of America was born — a republic with an elected president (George Washington, 1789).
  • A Constitution was written (1787) with a Bill of Rights guaranteeing freedoms (speech, religion, press).
  • Inspired the French Revolution (1789) and the 1798 Irish Rebellion.
  • Limitations: Slavery continued. Women could not vote. Native Americans were excluded.
Link to Ireland: The American Revolution directly inspired Wolfe Tone and the United Irishmen. The idea that people could overthrow an unfair government and create a republic gave Irish rebels hope and a model to follow.

4. The 1798 Rebellion

1791 – 1803
Inspired by the American and French revolutions, the United Irishmen attempted to overthrow British rule and establish an Irish republic. The rebellion was crushed, and Ireland was merged with Britain under the Act of Union (1800).

Background: Ireland in the 1790s

  • Ireland was ruled by the Irish Parliament in Dublin, but only Protestants (mainly Church of Ireland/Anglicans) could sit in it.
  • The Penal Laws discriminated against Catholics and Presbyterians: they couldn't vote, hold office, own land freely, or attend university.
  • The American Revolution (1776) and French Revolution (1789) spread ideas of liberty, equality, and republicanism.

The United Irishmen

  • Founded in Belfast in October 1791 by Theobald Wolfe Tone, Thomas Russell, and others.
  • A Dublin branch was set up the following month, led by Napper Tandy.
  • Aims: Unite Catholic, Protestant, and Dissenter (Presbyterian) to reform parliament and achieve Catholic emancipation. Eventually became revolutionary — seeking an Irish republic with French help.
  • Initially a legal, peaceful reform movement. After being banned in 1794, they became a secret, revolutionary organisation.

Wolfe Tone (1763–1798)

  • A Dublin-born Protestant lawyer. Believed the main problem in Ireland was English interference.
  • Published An Argument on Behalf of the Catholics of Ireland (1791).
  • Travelled to France to seek military help for an Irish rebellion.
  • 1796: Bantry Bay Expedition — 15,000 French troops under General Hoche sailed for Ireland but were scattered by storms. Never landed. A massive missed opportunity.
  • Captured in 1798 during a second French expedition. Sentenced to death. Died in prison (suicide or murder — debated).
  • Known as the "Father of Irish Republicanism."

The 1798 Rebellion

Before the Rising

  • The British government infiltrated the United Irishmen with spies and informers.
  • Key leaders like Lord Edward Fitzgerald were arrested before the rebellion began.
  • The government used harsh tactics: flogging, pitch-capping, half-hanging to terrorise the population and find hidden weapons.

The Rising (May–September 1798)

  • Leinster: The planned Dublin rising failed because leaders were captured. Scattered fighting in Kildare, Carlow, Meath, Wicklow.
  • Wexford: The most significant rising. Led by Father John Murphy, rebels won early battles at Oulart Hill and Enniscorthy, and set up a camp on Vinegar Hill.
  • Battle of Vinegar Hill (21 June 1798) — 20,000 British troops attacked. The rebels were defeated. This effectively ended the Wexford rising.
  • Ulster: Henry Joy McCracken led a rising in Antrim; Henry Munro led one in Down. Both were quickly defeated.
  • French Landing at Killala (August 1798) — General Humbert landed with 1,000 French troops in Mayo. Won the "Races of Castlebar" (British fled). Eventually defeated at Ballinamuck, Co. Longford.

Results of the 1798 Rebellion

  • 30,000+ people killed — mostly rebels and civilians.
  • Wolfe Tone captured and died in prison.
  • Britain decided direct rule was needed — led to the Act of Union (1800).
  • The Irish Parliament was abolished. Ireland was now governed directly from Westminster (London).
  • The promise of Catholic Emancipation was broken (King George III blocked it).
  • Robert Emmet's Rising (1803) — A small, failed attempt to revive the cause. Emmet was executed. His speech from the dock became famous in Irish nationalism.
Why 1798 Matters: It was the first attempt at an Irish republic uniting Catholic and Protestant. Its failure led to the Act of Union, which shaped Irish politics for the next 120 years and fuelled every subsequent independence movement.

5. The Great Famine

1845 – 1852
The Great Famine (An Gorta Mór) was the worst disaster in Irish history. A potato blight caused mass starvation and emigration, killing approximately 1 million people and forcing another 1 million to emigrate.

Background: Ireland Before the Famine

  • Ireland's population had grown rapidly — from ~5 million in 1800 to over 8 million by 1841.
  • Most people were poor tenant farmers renting small plots from landlords.
  • The potato was the main food source — cheap, nutritious, and grew well in Ireland. A family of six might eat 5–6 kg of potatoes per day.
  • Over-dependence on one crop made Ireland extremely vulnerable.
  • The Subdivision of land meant farms got smaller with each generation.

The Blight Arrives

  • In September 1845, a fungus called Phytophthora infestans (potato blight) arrived, probably from North America.
  • It turned potatoes into black, mushy, inedible rot — sometimes overnight.
  • 1845: About one-third of the crop destroyed.
  • 1846: Almost the entire crop destroyed. This was the worst year.
  • 1847: "Black '47" — less blight but almost nothing had been planted. Mass death and disease.
  • 1848–49: Blight returned. Famine continued.

Government Response

Under PM Robert Peel (Conservative, to June 1846)

  • Imported Indian corn (maize) from America. It was unpopular and hard to digest (called "Peel's Brimstone").
  • Set up public works schemes (road building) so people could earn money to buy food.
  • Repealed the Corn Laws (which kept grain prices high) to allow cheaper food imports. This cost him his job.

Under PM Lord John Russell (Whig, from June 1846)

  • Believed in laissez-faire economics — the government should NOT interfere with the market. Let trade sort itself out.
  • Also believed the famine was God's judgement on the Irish (providentialism).
  • Soup kitchens set up in 1847 — fed up to 3 million people daily at their peak. But closed after just 6 months.
  • Public works continued but wages were too low and work was too hard for starving people. Many died working on roads.
  • The Gregory Clause (1847) — Anyone holding more than a quarter-acre of land could not receive relief. Forced people to give up their land to get food.
  • Charles Trevelyan (head of Treasury) oversaw relief. He believed too much help would make the Irish "dependent." He ended food imports, saying the famine was "over."

Emigration & the Coffin Ships

  • Over 1 million people emigrated during the Famine, mainly to America, Canada, England, and Australia.
  • Coffin ships — Overcrowded, disease-ridden ships with appalling conditions. Many died en route from typhus, cholera, and dysentery.
  • Grosse Île (quarantine island near Quebec, Canada) — thousands died in quarantine after arriving.
  • Some landlords paid for tenants' passage to clear them off the land (assisted emigration).

Evictions & Workhouses

  • Tenants who couldn't pay rent were evicted. Their homes were "tumbled" (knocked down) so they couldn't return.
  • Workhouses were built under the Poor Law system. Conditions were deliberately harsh to discourage entry: families were separated, food was basic, work was hard.
  • Workhouses became overcrowded — became breeding grounds for disease. Many entered healthy and died inside.

Consequences of the Famine

  • Death toll: Approximately 1 million died from starvation and disease.
  • Emigration: Over 1 million emigrated during the Famine. Emigration continued for decades — population fell from 8.2 million (1841) to 4.4 million (1901).
  • Irish language decline: Irish-speaking areas (the West) were hardest hit. English became dominant.
  • Land reform: Led eventually to campaigns for tenant rights and the Land League (1879).
  • Anti-British feeling: Many Irish blamed Britain for the Famine. Fuelled nationalism, Fenianism, and the independence movement.
  • The Irish diaspora: Irish communities in America, Britain, and Australia became powerful political forces.
  • Marriage patterns changed: Later marriage, inheritance going to one son only (to avoid subdivision).
Exam Debate: Was the Famine genocide? Some historians argue Britain could have done far more — food was exported from Ireland throughout the Famine. Others say the British response was inadequate but not deliberate extermination. The exam may ask you to discuss both sides. Always use evidence: name policies, dates, and outcomes.

How the Topics Connect

The exam loves questions that link topics together. Here are the key connections:

Reformation → Plantations The Reformation created the Catholic–Protestant divide. England became Protestant; Ireland stayed Catholic. Plantations were partly about spreading Protestantism in Ireland and controlling the Catholic population.
Plantations → 1798 & Famine Plantations created the landlord system: Protestant landlords, Catholic tenants. This land system drove resentment behind 1798 and made the poor dependent on small plots of potato land — a direct cause of the Famine.
American Revolution → 1798 The American Revolution proved a colony could overthrow its ruler. Its ideas of liberty and equality directly inspired Wolfe Tone and the United Irishmen. The French, having helped America, were also willing to help Ireland.
1798 → Act of Union → Famine The 1798 Rebellion failed, leading to the Act of Union (1800). When the Famine struck, Ireland had no parliament of its own — decisions were made in London by politicians who often didn't understand or prioritise Irish suffering.
